Bio

I graduated with a B.Sc. in Physics/Astronomy from Florida International University where I researched micro-variability (Δt~hrs) of blazars with Dr. James Webb and worked with astronomical instrumentation at our on campus observatory the Stocker AstroScience Center and through the SARA Observatories.

I'm currently a T.A. at FIU, leading an exoplanet transit survey, and working in Dr. Hebin Li’s lab.

I will be applying to graduate programs for the upcoming year.

Research

  • Blazar Micro-Variability

    Blazars are active galactic nuclei with jets of relativistic material pointed towards our line of sight. This gives us an unobstructed view down the jet towards the heart of the galaxy. Their observed luminosity has been shown to be temporally variable on timescales of years to even hours. Below is an example of our data of blazar 0716+71 showing the ~hourly temporal fluctuations in magnitude.


    I worked with Dr. James Webb to understand the mechanisms causing the multifrequency short-term variations (micro-variability) via shock-waves propagating down the jet very near the SMBH. I gathered and reduced data from our numerous observatories on blazars for model fitting.

  • Exoplanet Detection and Characterization

    It’s incredible how we have achieved the ability to observe and study planets on other star systems. A few of the techniques used are radial velocity measurements of the host star, transits observed in the star luminosity, and even directly imaging the system with the help of adaptive optics. At FIU, I have started a small survey to test the sensitivity of our 0.6-meter telescope in detecting transits with ΔF/F ≈1-2% dips in luminosity. Below is data taken with our 0.6-m telescope of GJ3470.


    I’m interested in exoplanet detection methods, the connection between exoplanet orbital parameters and composition in relation to the type of host star, and how stellar evolution affects the life of the exoplanets.

  • Multidimensional Coherent Spectroscopy

    Spectroscopy has historically been a very powerful tool for studying how matter interacts with light. Ultrafast spectroscopy utilizing coherent collinear and non-collinear FWM can probe the evolution of coupling dynamics of materials and reveal information in the form of 2-dimensional spectroscopy. Below is a simulated 2D non-rephasing spectrum of a four-level energy band structure.


    I'm currently shadowing a graduate student to learn how to build, calibrate, and eventually take data with a collinear FWM spectrometer.

  • Ion Propulsion Engines

    Ion thrusters are lucrative for long duration space flights as they have shown to be incredibly efficient compared to contemporary chemical rockets.


    For a period of time, I worked on designing an acceleration grid ion thruster with a modular architecture.
